The idea in one line

Most "AI build" demos start with a finished idea. I start with nothing — eight user interviews and a question — and show the audience the real product loop: let AI synthesise the research, turn it into a falsifiable bet, build the tool by describing it, make it genuinely safe with guardrails and an automated test, and ship it to production. Then the room becomes the test. The point isn't that AI is fast — it's that speed is now free, so judgment is the job.

The product I build live: QuickFit

A dead-simple tool where you type what you've actually got right now — your time, energy, and equipment — and get one short workout you can start immediately. No programs, no quizzes, no gym. It's relatable, it's safe to put in front of a crowd, and everyone in the room can try it on their phone.

What happens on stage

A 4-minute framing, then a 30-minute live build in five beats.

  • ~4 min

    Opening — the framing

    A short, punchy deck: the three real jobs of product, the Confidence Line mental model, and why AI collapses the old build chain. Sets the lens for everything that follows.

  • 0–6:30

    Research

    Eight raw interviews on screen. AI synthesises them live into themes and the single biggest pain — in seconds.

  • 6:30–10

    Hypothesis

    Turn that pain into a bet I can be wrong about, and name the riskiest assumption out loud: will people actually use it?

  • 10–19

    Prototype

    Build the working app by describing it to the AI agent in plain English — then test it live on screen.

  • 19–22:30

    Make it safe

    Add input/output guardrails plus a medical safety cut-out, and run an automated eval that proves it — green across the board.

  • 22:30–30

    Ship → real users

    Deploy to production, put a QR code on screen, and the whole room builds workouts that fill a shared live wall — validating the bet in real time.
